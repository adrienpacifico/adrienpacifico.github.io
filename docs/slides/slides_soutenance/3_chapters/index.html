# Three Chapters: 1. Monthly Income Tax Frequency 2. A Direct Measure of Inefficiency within Couples 3. Rich Households Taxable Income
## 1. Monthly Income Tax Frequency: ### Theoretical and Empirical Investigations (Joint work with O. Bargain & A. Trannoy)
### What is the impact of the frequency of the tax on households' welfare?
### I. Theoretical analysis #### Hypothesis : 1. A concave utility function in disposable income. 2. Inter-temporally additive 2. Exogenous income 3. Myopic/No savings 4. A convex income tax schedule
### Formally compare two tax systems: Anual basis with monthly payments: $$y_t - \frac{G(\sum_1^{12} y_t)}{12} $$ Monthy basis with monthly payments : $$y_t - \frac{G(12 \times y_t)}{12} $$ > Income smoothing vs. Increase in tax liability
Annual basis compensated with monthly payments: $$ y_t - \frac{1}{12+\lambda} \; G(12y_t) $$ Income Averaging (Vickrey 1939): $$ {g_{t}^{V}}= \frac{t}{T} G\left(T\frac{\sum_{p=0}^{t}y_{p}}{t}\right) -\sum_{p=0}^{t-1}g_{p}^{V}$$
Theoretical result: The compensated scheme is Pareto improving compared to the equal payment scheme.

## 2. A Direct Measure of Inefficiency within Couples (Joint work with O. Bargain, D. Echevin & N. Moreau)
How do cohabiting couples allocate their children between their two tax-units?
### Context: - Cohabitants with children constitute two tax units - Children can be allocated between the two tax returns - There exists an optimal allocation in order to minimize the tax
Exemple: père 17 968, mère 41 222 Why would cohabitants do not choose the best allocation ?
## Analysis Data: *Échantillong Démographique Permanent* (EDP) 1. From 2 million tax households in 2013, I select 193 000 cohabiting couples, from which 52 000 have all their children identified as biological children in the civil registry. 2. Compute the income tax for each of the two tax units in each household based on children allocation.
25% of households do not optimize their disposable income by not choosing the optimal allocation. 8% of households do not optimize their disposable income up to 1% of their pre-tax income.
# Explanations: I. The Dynamics II. Cooperation
## The Dynamics: We observe that inertia is a very strong determinant of optimization. Indeed households rarely change their allocation whether they should or not.
## Cooperation: Multinomial Logit: Couples that do not optimize for large values of their taxable income, are more likely to separate the year after, once controlled for a large set of covariates. Conversely, couples that do optimize are more likely to marry or start a civil union.
![](./images/MNL_cooperation.png)
This suggests that non-cooperation is one of the derterminants of the non respect of efficiency inside households.
## 3. Rich households taxable income
How do rich French households react to the lowering of the maximum child tax break with respect to their taxable income?

Context:

  1. The French tax system works such that the child tax break is increasing with income.
  2. However, the tax gain a household can derive from a child is bounded by a ceiling.
  3. The ceiling per child has been lowered from 2336€ to 1500€ between 2011 and 2014. That per child ceiling is twice the baseline amount for the third child and over.

Households have been affected in three ways:

  1. Households for which the tax advantage per child was lower than 1500 euros are unaffected.
  2. Households for which the tax advantage per child was between 1500 euros and 2336 euros faced a change in their marginal tax rate.
  3. Households for which the tax advantage per child was over 2336 euros faced a lump sum loss in disposable income of 836€ per child.
Implication: As the tax advantage increases with income, there exists an income threshold at which a household will attain the ceiling. |# of children |2011| 2013| |------------|----------|----------|--------|--------| |1 |63,083 |58,380 |2 |73,539 |63,396 |3 |94,343 |73,844 |4 |115,142 |84,095 |5 |135,922 |94,508 |6 |150,684 |104,691

# Conclusion
The first chapter looks at a subject that has not been much studied. It gives hindsight about the impact of the frequency of the tax by suggesting that welfare gains are possible. It also provides a methodology to assess quantitatively the impact that a change in the frequency of the tax could have on taxpayers' welfare.
The second chapter shows that households do not always respect efficiency.
The third chapter tries to assess the behavioral reactions associated with a tax reform. It does so by relying on a "new" methodology that was supposed to solve many existing caveats in the litterature. This methology also tries to disentangle the income effect and the substitution effect. However, the common trend assumptions are not respected, and once corrected for the trend before the reform, the results tend to show a null effect of the reform.
